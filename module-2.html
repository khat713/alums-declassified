Campus Resources

Financial aid, tutoring, counseling, food pantries — and how to actually walk into an office for the first time.

Learning Objectives

By the End of This Module, You Will Be Able To:

  1. Identify at least six campus resources across academic, financial, and health/wellbeing categories.
  2. Explain the purpose of each resource and describe a realistic scenario for using it.
  3. Draft a professional email to a campus office requesting help or information.

Focus on making resources feel approachable — many first-gen students avoid offices because they don't know how they work or feel like they don't belong there.

Learning Activity

Activity: Draft an Email to a Professor

Instructions: Write a short email (3–5 sentences) to a professor you've never met, asking to visit during office hours because you have a question about the course syllabus. Use the format covered in the lesson.

Things to include: a subject line, a greeting, your name and course section, the reason you're writing, and a polite close.

Why this matters: Knowing how to email a professor is a skill most first-gen students aren't taught. You'll use it within the first two weeks of class.
Assessment

Applied Task: Personal Campus Resource Map

Instructions: Using the provided template, identify at least 6 campus resources at your specific school across these categories:
  • Academic support (tutoring, writing center, advising)
  • Financial resources (financial aid, emergency aid, food pantry)
  • Health and wellbeing (counseling, campus health)
For each resource, record: name of office, location, contact info, and one realistic scenario in which you'd actually use it.
